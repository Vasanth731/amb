Planetary Sciences

The lifecycle of a star begins in a vast stellar nebula, a cloud of gas and dust spanning about 200 trillion kilometers. Under gravity, regions within the nebula collapse to form a protostar, roughly 100 million kilometers in size, which continues to contract and heat up until nuclear fusion begins, marking its transition into a main sequence star of about 1 million kilometers in diameter. As the star exhausts its hydrogen fuel, it expands into either a red giant (around 100 million kilometers) or, if it is much more massive, a red supergiant (about 1000 million kilometers). A red giant sheds its outer layers to create a planetary nebula, stretching up to 1000 billion kilometers, leaving behind a dense white dwarf of about 10,000 kilometers, which eventually cools into a black dwarf of similar size. In contrast, a red supergiant ends its life in a dramatic supernova explosion, after which its core collapses into either an dense neutron star, only about 15 kilometers wide, or, if massive enough, a black hole with a radius of roughly 50 kilometers.
