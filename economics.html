Economics

Economics is the study of how people, businesses, and governments make choices when resources are limited. At its core, economics asks how societies produce goods and services, distribute them, and decide who gets to consume them. A useful starting point is the concept of scarcity: because time, money, labor, and natural resources are finite, individuals and societies must make trade-offs. Economics is often divided into microeconomics, which examines the decisions of individuals and firms, and macroeconomics, which studies the economy as a whole, including topics such as inflation, unemployment, economic growth, and government policy. Key concepts include supply and demand, markets, prices, incentives, competition, and opportunity cost—the value of the next best alternative that is given up when making a choice.

Economics is not just about money; it is a framework for understanding human behavior and decision-making. It helps explain why prices change, why some industries grow while others decline, how governments use taxes and spending to influence the economy, and how international trade affects countries. By learning economics, you gain tools to analyze everyday issues such as housing costs, wages, business decisions, and public policies. Rather than providing simple answers, economics encourages systematic thinking about incentives, trade-offs, and consequences, helping citizens, consumers, and policymakers make more informed decisions in a world of limited resources and competing goals.
