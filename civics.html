Civics

Civics is the study of how societies govern themselves and how citizens participate in public life. It explains the structure and functions of government, the rights and responsibilities of citizens, and the rules that help communities live together peacefully. A good place to start is understanding the three main branches of government found in many democracies: the legislative branch, which makes laws; the executive branch, which implements and enforces laws; and the judicial branch, which interprets laws and resolves disputes. Civics also covers concepts such as constitutions, elections, political parties, local government, and the rule of law. The central idea is that governments derive their authority from the people and are accountable to them.

As a citizen, civics helps you understand not only your rights—such as freedom of speech, voting rights, and legal protections—but also your responsibilities, including obeying laws, paying taxes, serving on juries where applicable, and participating in community affairs. Learning civics enables you to evaluate public policies, follow current events, engage in informed discussions, and make thoughtful decisions during elections. Rather than focusing on politicians or political debates alone, civics teaches how institutions work, why they exist, and how ordinary people can influence decisions that affect their communities and countries. It is essentially the study of citizenship and the practical workings of democracy and government.
