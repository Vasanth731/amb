Theology

The Perspective

Theology in my perspective is the study of how people throughout history have understood God. We ourselves are part of creation, and therefore a created mind cannot fully comprehend the Creator, but we can attempt to understand through reflection and study. The oldest writings related to this tradition are the Dead Sea Scrolls, which show how the Hebrews interpreted their Creator. According to this narrative, God created the universe, a dynamic system of space, time, and matter. Humanity began with Adam, whose fall was followed by generations leading to Noah and the great flood. Even after the flood there were further transgressions. Later Abraham, descended from Terah in the line of Shem, was called by God to follow Him. Through Isaac and Jacob, whose twelve sons formed the tribes of Israel, the story continues. Joseph became the right hand of Pharaoh and saved his family during famine, though later generations fell into captivity. Through Moses the Exodus occurred, and the nation of Israel was formed, guided by judges, prophets, and kings. All these events were seen as preparing the way for the Messiah, Christ Jesus, believed by Christians to be the Word of God who became flesh, fulfilled prophecies, lived a perfect life, died to save humanity, and rose again. After his ascension, his followers spread this message and many became martyrs. They formed the early centers of Christianity in Jerusalem, Antioch, Alexandria, Constantinople, and Rome. Over time various interpretations and denominations emerged as people developed different theological and social understandings of the church.

Judaism

Judaism is one of the oldest Abrahamic faiths and shares the early creation narrative and covenant traditions dating to around 2000 BCE. It centers on the covenant between the one God, often referred to as YHWH, and the descendants of Abraham. This covenant was formalized through Moses and the giving of the Torah at Sinai. Central beliefs include the declaration of the Shema, “Hear O Israel, the Lord our God, the Lord is One,” and the observance of 613 mitzvot or commandments concerning ethics, ritual, and justice. Jewish scripture includes the Tanakh, composed of the Torah, the Prophets, and the Writings, as well as the Oral Torah preserved in the Mishnah and Talmud. Jewish life involves rituals such as circumcision, bar or bat mitzvah, marriage traditions, and mourning practices. Weekly Shabbat observance and festivals such as Rosh Hashanah, Yom Kippur, Passover, and Hanukkah shape religious life. Over time several branches developed, including Orthodox, Conservative, Reform, and Reconstructionist traditions. Jewish thought also includes historical schools such as the teachings associated with Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel.

Islam

Islam originated in seventh-century Arabia with the Prophet Muhammad, who is believed to have received revelations from God through the angel Gabriel. These revelations were compiled in the Quran, considered the final and preserved scripture in Islam. Central to Islamic belief is Tawhid, the absolute oneness of God, along with belief in angels, earlier scriptures, prophets from Adam to Muhammad, the Day of Judgment, and divine destiny. The life and sayings of Muhammad recorded in hadith literature serve as guidance alongside the Quran. After Muhammad’s death in 632 CE, the community divided over leadership, leading to the Sunni and Shia traditions. Islamic practice centers on the Five Pillars: the declaration of faith, daily prayers, charity, fasting during Ramadan, and pilgrimage to Mecca. Islamic law, known as Sharia, guides ethical and social life. Throughout history the Islamic world contributed greatly to science, medicine, mathematics, and philosophy during the Islamic Golden Age. Mystical traditions such as Sufism emphasize spiritual experience and closeness to the divine.

Hinduism

Hinduism developed over thousands of years in the Indian subcontinent and does not have a single founder. It encompasses a wide range of philosophies, rituals, and traditions. Central ideas include Brahman as the ultimate reality, Atman as the individual soul, karma as the law of action and consequence, samsara as the cycle of rebirth, and moksha as liberation from that cycle. Hindu thought includes many deities and traditions, such as the Trimurti of Brahma, Vishnu, and Shiva, as well as various forms of the divine feminine. Sacred texts include the Vedas, the Upanishads, the epics Mahabharata and Ramayana, and later Puranic literature. Paths to spiritual growth include knowledge, devotion, action, and meditation. Religious practice often involves rituals known as puja, temple worship, pilgrimages, and festivals such as Diwali and Holi. Hindu traditions have also influenced philosophical and ethical ideas such as nonviolence, which shaped figures like Mahatma Gandhi.

Buddhism

Buddhism began in the fifth century BCE with Siddhartha Gautama, who later became known as the Buddha or “the Enlightened One.” After encountering suffering in the world, he taught a path aimed at understanding and overcoming it. His teachings are summarized in the Four Noble Truths, which explain suffering, its origin in craving, its cessation, and the path to its end through the Noble Eightfold Path. Buddhism also teaches the impermanence of all things, the absence of a permanent self, and the importance of wisdom, ethical conduct, and meditation. The goal is nirvana, liberation from the cycle of rebirth. Different traditions developed over time, including Theravada in Southeast Asia, Mahayana in East Asia, and Vajrayana in Tibet. Buddhist communities are centered around the Sangha, the monastic and lay community that preserves and practices the teachings.

African Diasporic Religions

African diasporic religions developed from indigenous African traditions that spread through the transatlantic slave trade and later blended with elements of Christianity and Islam. Traditions such as Vodou in Haiti, Santería in Cuba, and Candomblé in Brazil reflect this blending. Many of these traditions view the universe as interconnected through a spiritual realm, the human realm, and the ancestral realm. Deities and spirits are honored through rituals, music, dance, offerings, and festivals. Ancestors play an important role, and community identity is often preserved through these spiritual practices. These traditions emphasize harmony between people, nature, and the spiritual world.

Shinto

Shinto is the indigenous spiritual tradition of Japan and is often described as “the way of the kami,” referring to spiritual beings or forces present in nature and in ancestors. It has no single founder or central scripture, though early myths appear in texts such as the Kojiki and Nihongi. According to these myths, the cosmos emerged from primordial chaos and the first heavenly deities appeared, followed by generations of divine beings including Izanagi and Izanami who created the islands of Japan and many other kami. Nature itself is considered sacred, and humans live alongside these spirits. Ritual purification practices and shrine worship are central to Shinto life. Kami are believed to inhabit natural places and sacred objects, and harmony with them is maintained through respect, offerings, and rituals performed by shrine priests.

Daoism

Daoism emerged in ancient China and is associated with thinkers such as Laozi and Zhuangzi. The central idea is the Dao, the fundamental way or principle underlying the universe. Daoist teachings encourage harmony with natural patterns rather than forcing outcomes. Concepts such as yin and yang describe the dynamic balance of opposites, while qi represents the vital energy that flows through all things. Practices include meditation, philosophy, martial arts, and traditional healing methods, all aimed at living in harmony with the natural order.

Confucianism

Confucianism began with the teachings of Confucius in ancient China around the sixth century BCE. It focuses less on theology and more on ethical and social harmony. Important values include filial piety, humaneness, and proper conduct within society. Confucius taught that a stable and moral society depends on respect, education, and the cultivation of virtue. His teachings were later compiled in the Analects and became deeply influential in East Asian philosophy and governance.

Atheism

Atheism is not a religion but a philosophical position that rejects belief in gods or supernatural beings. It emphasizes evidence, reason, and human-centered ethics. Although skepticism toward gods appears in ancient philosophy, atheism became more prominent during the Enlightenment and with the growth of modern science. Some forms of atheism actively deny the existence of deities, while others simply lack belief. Many atheists adopt ethical frameworks based on humanism, emphasizing compassion, human rights, and rational inquiry rather than divine authority.

Other Beliefs and Observations

Throughout history many cultures practiced polytheism, believing in multiple gods associated with natural forces. Monuments such as Stonehenge or ancient Egyptian sun worship reflect early attempts to understand cosmic order. Some traditions developed ideas of a single supreme force governing the universe, while others focused on interactions between the human and spirit worlds. Modern scientific studies have even examined how the brain responds during meditation or prayer, suggesting that belief and faith can influence neurological activity. These observations show that humanity’s search for meaning and understanding of the divine has taken many different forms across cultures and time.
